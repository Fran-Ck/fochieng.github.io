SYSTEMS DYNAMICS AND SYSTEMS THINKING IN THE EDUCATION SECTOR

There is a growing recognition of the complexity that underlies the Academic sector and its management and with it the need for sound Education policies and decision making that embraces this complexity.

Decision makers, more than ever are required to integrate scientific and expert-based evidence into decision-making. Complexity can hinder evidence based-decision making by slowing and weakening the ways in which evidence is collected, interpreted and translated into action. As it is the current analytical tools used to support evidence-based decision making such as statistical modelling, have their shortcomings in terms of inability  to explain causation and effect of nonlinear interactions and feedback on the behavior of complex systems. In light of this, there is a need to provide decision makers with the tools necessary to understand complex Educational issues as part of implementing more scientific decision-making processes.

System Dynamics(SD) modelling is a problem-oriented modelling approach pioneered by Jay Forrester in the late 1950’s with the aim of helping corporate managers better understand industrial problems. SD involves causal mapping and the development of computer simulation to understand systems behavior. Policy and scenarios options are then systematically tested to answer “What-if” questions, this gives a chance to the policy makers to experiment with their decisions before implementing in the real world. The decision-experimentation creates a learning environment in which policy makers gain a better understanding of how the system will respond to their decisions and the potentially unintended consequences of the decisions.

Traditionally, problem solving tends to approach complex problem by breaking them into their component parts and examining them separately while SD centres on the idea that problems exist due to the interactions, feedback loops and material and information delays among components parts within a system, In summary, SD focuses on the relationships between parts rather than focusing on separate parts in isolation. In addition to that, SD draws strongly on the concept of endogeneity, this means that it seeks to find explanations for systems behavior by understanding the internal structure of a system rather than focusing on factors external to the system.

For example, in trying to understand the problem on Universities and Colleges releasing “Half-baked” graduates into the labour market, according to [1], there are so many variables that can be attributed to this phenomenon, such as: Too much theory in universities,few academic hours, some universities admitting unqualified students despite the stipulated minimum requirement, just to mention but a few. An endogenous perspective would be take the view that the reason for this would be a combination of systematic factors such as lectures’s student load,

Training and facilitator practices, Institution’s culture etc, and thus the solution lies within understanding how these factors contribute to “Half-Baked” graduates then working on policies that reduce this problem.

In conclusion, in a dynamic world and the ever changing state of technology, Academic Policies and  decisions must be made regardless of the complexity of Education systems and uncertainties about the future. This article suggests that System Dynamics be applied increasingly within the sector of Education since it has a potential when it comes to assisting decision makers understand and analyse complex Academic issues as well as predicting the outcomes of Educational policies and decisions. Current Decision making and Governance structures in Institutions of higher learning are not designed to integrate the multi-sectoral policy advice that SD can provide, therefore, the full potential is SD to change the way in which Education problems are managed cannot be realised unless the decision-making and governance structure of these institutions are collaborative and coordinated.
